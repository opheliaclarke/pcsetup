0 The big correction

Rev 1 of this document said the PRO 6000 "exhausts out the back — a good citizen." That is wrong, and it was the load-bearing assumption under the whole case choice. Here is what is actually true.

What Rev 1 assumedWhat is actually trueWhat it changes
PRO 6000 is a rear-exhaust blower; its heat leaves the case on its own. Double-flow-through. NVIDIA's own datasheet. Air in from beneath the card, out the top, into the case. AEC Magazine: it "raises internal chassis temperatures." The case must evacuate 600 W, not merely house it. Bottom intake + roof exhaust becomes mandatory.
The 5090 is a 4-slot card; sag is the main risk. Every liquid 5090 is a 2.5-slot card. The PRO 6000 is 2-slot — but extended height, 137 mm vs the standard 112 mm. Sag is a minor problem, not a major one. Card height clearance becomes something to check.
2nd PCIe slot is ~Gen4 ×4. PCI_E2 is PCIe 5.0 ×4, CPU-attached. And populating it drops PCI_E1 from ×16 to PCIe 5.0 ×8. Twice the bandwidth you planned for. Also: don't buy a Gen4 riser "because the slot is slow."
Corsair 9000D is the winner. It is 664 mm tall — 104 mm too tall to ever go under the desk, and ₹37,831 on Amazon with 2 units left. Eliminated on height alone.
📉
The one thing Rev 1 got right and is worth repeating: no NVLink, mismatched VRAM (96 GB vs 32 GB) and mismatched clocks. Use the cards as two independent workers — heavy fine-tuning on the 96 GB PRO 6000, inference / second job on the 5090. Not one merged 128 GB trainer.

2 Fan & radiator layout — this is where the performance is

The case is only half the answer. The PRO 6000 is a 600 W air-cooled card living in the same box as 775 W of radiator. Where you put those radiators decides whether it throttles.

⛔
Never put a radiator in the PRO 6000's intake path — not in the front-bottom, not on the shroud. GamersNexus measured a front-mounted rad costing an air-cooled GPU 7–9°C versus roof-exhaust. A builder with two PRO 6000s on Reddit hit exactly this and called it "a disaster."

The honest trade-off: the roof radiator is being fed case air that the PRO 6000 has already warmed, so the 5090's coolant will run a few degrees hotter than it would in a single-GPU build. That is the correct direction to lose: liquid cooling has enormous thermal headroom, and the air-cooled card has none. Protect the card that can actually throttle.

The fan curve is not optional

This is the most consistently reported finding across every owner running these cards hard: NVIDIA's stock VBIOS fan curve leaves the card at ~30% fan speed while it sits at 85–90°C, and it begins throttling around there. Measured on a 450 W-limited PRO 6000 under sustained LLM load:

3 Desk clearance — the crush problem

The desk is an Ergosphere Balanced Desk PRO 340 L-Shape Corner, triple-motor, bought frame-only. Its published "63 cm minimum" is measured to the top of the desktop — so the underside, the part that would hit your PC, bottoms out at roughly 605–615 mm.

⚠️
Anti-collision will not save the PC. Your frame is a LOGICDATA/Bosch stack, and LOGICDATA's manual states the downward trip force is ~40 kg plus the desk's own load before it reacts — enough to crush a radiator or crack glass first. Worse, the manual warns three times that anti-collision is disabled during any reset or calibration, and a reset drives the desk below its normal lowest point, past any limit you programmed.

Do all three of these

  • 1. Set a container stop at 700 mm. On LOGICDATA: drive the desk to the height you want, hold the "S" button for 10 seconds, listen for two clicks. That becomes the new lower limit. Check the label on your control box first — Ergosphere ships both LOGICDATA and JieCang controllers and the procedure differs.
  • 2. Fit physical steel hard-stops under the frame, cut to 700 mm. Two posts, one under each leg-end — never one in the middle of the span. Size them for ~2,500 N: the motors push down as hard as they lift, and your frame is rated 120–150 kg. Rigid steel or hardwood only — never foam or a rolled towel, because LOGICDATA explicitly lists "soft object over a long time" as a case where anti-collision fails to trigger at all.
  • 3. Never run a desk reset with the PC underneath. Pull it out first, every single time. Any error code or power cut can force one.
📐
What that gets you: a 700 mm stop over a 545 mm case leaves 155 mm of air above the PC for its roof exhaust — close to the 200 mm rule of thumb, and enough that it isn't re-breathing its own heat. With a 25 mm top, your desk still sits at 725 mm — a textbook sitting height — and the full standing range above it is untouched. You lose nothing.

4 Mounting the two cards

Rev 1 planned a vertical GPU kit plus a riser cable. Scrap both. They were sized for a 4-slot, 2 kg card that you are not buying, and the vertical mount would actively hurt you.

Slot spacingOn the X870E Carbon, PCI_E1 and PCI_E2 sit 4 slot-pitches apart (~81 mm centre-to-centre). A 2-slot PRO 6000 up top leaves two empty slot positions — about 40 mm of clear air — before the 5090. That is better separation than most riser setups achieve.
Link speedsPRO 6000 in PCI_E1 → PCIe 5.0 ×8. 5090 in PCI_E2 → PCIe 5.0 ×4, CPU-attached. No M.2 or SATA port is lost. Populating PCI_E2 is what drops PCI_E1 from ×16 to ×8 — unavoidable on this board, and fine for two independent workers.
No vertical mountVertical-mounting the PRO 6000 turns its intake face toward the glass panel and starves it. It is a flow-through card; it needs a clear path from the shroud fans below. Also: the Lian Li VG4-4 V2 isn't buyable in India — one retailer lists it out of stock, and the only reachable copy is a grey-market import at ~2.3× MSRP.
No riserBoth cards seat natively. A riser adds a failure point and buys nothing.
SagReal but minor at 2 slots. A plain adjustable GPU support stick under the PRO 6000 is enough.
Power cableThe PRO 6000's 12V-2×6 connector is on the top middle edge of the card. Budget for an angled 12V-2×6 adapter or you will fight the side panel.
BIOSEnable Above 4G Decoding (mandatory for the 96 GB card's BAR) and Resizable BAR. Connect the board's PCIE_PWR1 supplemental 8-pin when running two GPUs.
🩺
Watch the motherboard, not just the GPUs. The best-documented failure in multi-GPU AI rigs is not a cooked card — it's the board. One builder chased daily hard lockups for weeks: the PCIe retimer chips hit their 90°C alarm and crashed the PCIe fabric, while the GPUs, which don't throttle until ~95°C, reported themselves perfectly happy. Another lost his onboard NIC to heat. Monitor board/VRM temps from day one.

Power-limit both GPUs (do this on day one)

For AI training, capping power barely dents throughput but slashes heat. Run after every reboot (or add to a startup script). Tune the wattages to taste.

power-limit.sh · run as admin/sudo
# See current limits & live draw
nvidia-smi -q -d POWER

# Cap each GPU (index 0 and 1). Adjust watts to your cards.
nvidia-smi -i 0 -pl 480   # RTX PRO 6000  (from 600W)
nvidia-smi -i 1 -pl 460   # RTX 5090      (from 575W)

# Watch temps under sustained load — target <80°C
nvidia-smi -q -d TEMPERATURE

# The stock fan curve idles at ~30% even at 85-90C. Override it.
# Linux: use LACT (github.com/ilya-zlobintsev/LACT) or a pynvml daemon.

🔌 Two power things people forget in India

  • Dedicated 16A circuit straight from the DB for the PC + AC. Don't share with the rest of the room. ~1.4kW PC + ~1.8kW (2-ton AC) ≈ 3.2kW continuous.
  • An online (double-conversion) UPS, ~3kVA. Training runs for hours — one power cut = lost progress + a hard yank on two ₹-heavy GPUs. Non-negotiable for a 24×7 training goal.

A 180 sq ft room normally wants ~1.5 ton of AC for its own load (walls, sun, people). The PC adds only ~0.4 ton on top. Go 2-ton, 5-star inverter — inverter is essential, because it modulates for continuous duty instead of hammering on/off.

💡
The real comfort issue is recirculation, not total BTU. Point the PC's exhaust — now the roof radiator and the rear fan — toward the AC's return, and sit upstream so cold air reaches you before it crosses the PC. A hot pocket at your chair is an airflow-direction problem, not a bigger-AC problem.

💸 Budget the running cost (it's real)

PC (~1.4kW) + 2-ton AC (~1.8kW) running 24×7 ≈ ~3.2 kW continuous ≈ ~77 units/day. At ₹8/unit that's roughly ₹600/day ≈ ₹18,000/month. Power-limiting, and only running training jobs when you need them, cuts this meaningfully.

🔊
Noise, honestly. Owners of these cards report significant coil whine — "I'd lose my mind if I had to work next to it all day." Two flagship GPUs at full fan, plus a pump, plus an AC, is loud. Worth knowing before it lives under your desk.

8 Order of operations

  • Buy the Antec Flux Pro. Measure its height with feet on arrival — you're relying on 545 mm.
  • Read the label on the desk's control box: LOGICDATA or JieCang. Set the container stop to 700 mm.
  • Fit the two steel hard-stop posts under the desk frame. Test by holding the down button.
  • Get the dedicated 16A circuit + 2-ton inverter AC installed.
  • Build: PRO 6000 in PCI_E1, 5090 in PCI_E2. LT720 front-intake, 5090 rad roof-exhaust, 3 fans in the PSU shroud.
  • BIOS: Above 4G Decoding on, Resizable BAR on, PCIE_PWR1 connected.
  • Verify link widths with GPU-Z — expect ×8 on the PRO 6000, ×4 on the 5090.
  • Install the fan-curve daemon and the power-limit script before the first sustained load.
  • Run a 2-hour training job. Watch GPU temps (<80°C) and motherboard/VRM temps.
  • Slide the PC under the desk. Position so the roof exhaust vents toward the AC return, not your chair.
