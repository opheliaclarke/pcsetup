0 The three facts everything else follows from

Every decision below traces back to these. Get them straight and the build designs itself.

The factWhy it's trueWhat it forces
The PRO 6000 is not a blower. Double-flow-through (NVIDIA's datasheet): it draws air in from beneath the card and vents up into the case. AEC Magazine: it "raises internal chassis temperatures." Only the 300 W Max-Q is a rear blower. Feed it fresh air from below and evacuate 600 W out the top. This is why the V3000's floor intake wins.
The 5090 is a ROG Astral LC. A 2.5-slot card (288 × 154 × 48 mm) plus its own thick 360 mm radiator — 400 × 120 × 65 mm (ASUS uses an extra-fat 38 mm core), on 600 mm tubes. The build must swallow a chunky radiator — trivial in a super-tower, tight in anything smaller.
The 2nd slot is PCIe 5.0 ×4. On the X870E Carbon, PCI_E2 is Gen5 ×4, CPU-attached; populating it drops PCI_E1 to Gen5 ×8. Both direct to the CPU. Plenty for two independent workers — a 96 GB card that keeps its job in VRAM barely uses the link.
📉
Use the cards as two independent workers — heavy fine-tuning on the 96 GB PRO 6000, inference / a second job on the 5090. Not one merged 128 GB trainer: no NVLink, mismatched VRAM (96 vs 32 GB) and clocks make a split model painful and slow.

1 The case

You own both GPUs, you sit at a 32 in desk, and Ergosphere can lock the desk's minimum height. That combination reopens the one case that's genuinely ideal for these parts.

🧩
The thing that was worrying you — settled. You're not cramming three big objects into the GPU area. It's two cards in the PCIe slots plus two flat radiators bolted to the panels. A radiator is a thin panel on hoses, not a third box. In a super-tower like the V3000 there's room to spare.
★ The build

Lian Li V3000 Plus — super-tower

678.5 (D) × 279 (W) × 674 mm (H) · 8 PCIe slots · mesh front/top/floor · built-in bottom dust filter · rated for up to 3× 480 mm rads + a 360 & 16 fans

  • ＋ The best possible feed for your flow-through 6000. A filtered floor intake plus a fan bank on the above-PSU shelf push fresh air straight up into the underside of the card — a two-stage feed that's essentially purpose-built for a bottom-breathing GPU. Nothing else does this as directly.
  • ＋ Both radiators fit with room to spare. The Astral LC's fat 65 mm rad goes on the front (the case is 678 mm deep — thickness is a non-issue there); the LT720 goes on top. Rated for far more radiator than you'll use.
  • ＋ The room you wanted. 589 mm GPU clearance, 8 slots, 220 mm PSU bay (the AX1600i drops in), a built-in 2-way anti-sag GPU holder, and a separate rear cable chamber. The build will never feel cramped.
  • ＋ It fits under the desk — because you sit at 32 in and the desk locks its minimum above the case (see Desk Lock). Height stopped being the blocker the moment Ergosphere confirmed the lock survives a reset.
  • － Ships with zero fans. Budget a full set (~7–8× 140 mm case fans on top of the fans that come on your two radiators). Factor that into the price.
  • － It's a heavy beast — well over 20 kg empty, a lot more built. Hence the wheeled pedestal. And its only dust filter is the floor one, so keep that clean.

No independent lab has thermal-tested this exact case, but the design is unambiguous: mesh intakes on three faces and a direct under-GPU feed. For a bottom-breathing 600 W card, that's the right architecture.

Why not the others

  • ASUS ROG Hyperion GR701 ("the 701") — the other super-tower you looked at. Ruled out on architecture, not size: it has no floor fan mount (a solid PSU cover sits under the card), so it can't feed the flow-through 6000 from below. Wrong shape for this GPU.
  • Corsair 9000D — a good case, but same story: no floor feed, and no reason to pick it over the V3000 now that the desk lock solves height.
  • Antec Flux Pro / Corsair 6500D — both fit and both are fine, but they're the smaller answers. You chose room, and the V3000 feeds the 6000 more directly.

2 Fan & radiator layout — where the performance is

The PRO 6000 is a 600 W air-cooled card sharing a box with ~755 W of radiator. Where those radiators go decides whether it throttles. The rule is simple: fresh air below the 6000, radiators everywhere else.

⛔
Never put a radiator on the floor or the above-PSU shelf under the 6000 — those two banks are fresh-air fans only. GamersNexus measured a radiator in an air-cooled GPU's intake path costing 7–9°C. A builder with two PRO 6000s on Reddit did exactly that and called it "a disaster."

The nice part of this layout: the bigger heat source (the 5090, ~575 W) gets fresh front air for its coolant, and the small one (the CPU, ~180 W) takes the warmed top air. You're spending the thermal penalty exactly where it's cheapest.

The fan curve is not optional

The single most consistent finding from owners running these cards hard: NVIDIA's stock VBIOS leaves the card at ~30% fan while it sits at 85–90°C, and it throttles around there. Measured on a 450 W-limited PRO 6000 under sustained LLM load:

Ergosphere confirmed it: the minimum height can be set as a default, and it survives a reset. That's the key — a soft handset "container stop" gets wiped when the desk resets (which drives it all the way down). A firmware minimum that persists through a reset is a real, reliable guard. This is now your primary protection.

Set the lock to match your final stack height

The lock has to sit above case + pedestal + a 50 mm air gap. Because the pedestal adds height, the exact number depends on how tall you build it. With a 25 mm tabletop:

Pedestal heightCase + pedestalSet the desk minimum toGap at your 32" working height
None674 mm29.5 in~114 mm
1 in (25 mm) recommended699 mm30.5 in~89 mm
2 in (50 mm)724 mm31.5 in~63 mm

Simplest foolproof method: assemble the case on its pedestal, measure the true top-of-case height with a tape, add ~50 mm, add your tabletop thickness — and set the lock there. Don't trust the handset's on-screen number; measure the physical height.

Two backups (cheap insurance)

  • Physical steel hard-stops under the frame, cut to the same lock height. The firmware lock is now solid, so these are belt-and-suspenders — but on a rig this expensive, a steel post that no glitch can push past is worth the small cost. Rigid steel or hardwood, one under each leg-end, never foam.
  • Still pull the PC out before any deliberate reset/recalibration. The lock survives a reset, but anti-collision is off during one, so give it clearance whenever you service the desk.
📐
What you end up with: at your 32 in working height there's a big gap over the case (~90 mm with a 1 in pedestal); at the locked minimum, ~50 mm; and the desk physically cannot go lower than that. Your standing range above is untouched. Crush risk: gone.

4 Mounting the two cards

Both cards seat natively — no vertical kit, no riser. Here's the placement that matters.

Slot order do thisPRO 6000 in the LOWER slot (PCI_E2); 5090 Astral LC in the TOP slot (PCI_E1). The lower slot sits right over the floor + above-PSU intake fans, so the flow-through 6000 gets the fresh-air feed. The liquid 5090 doesn't need it. Opposite of the "big card up top" habit — and correct here, because feeding the flow-through card is the whole game.
Link speedsPRO 6000 → PCIe 5.0 ×4, 5090 → PCIe 5.0 ×8. Both CPU-attached; no M.2 or SATA lost. The ×4 barely matters for a 96 GB card that keeps its job in VRAM, and it buys the better air feed. Only reconsider if the 6000 constantly streams from system RAM / NVMe offload.
Slot spacingThe two slots sit 4 pitches apart (~81 mm centre-to-centre) — ~two empty positions of clear air between the cards. Better separation than most riser setups.
SagUse the V3000's built-in 2-way anti-sag GPU holder. At 2 slots the cards aren't heavy, so this is plenty — no aftermarket bracket needed.
Power cableThe 6000's 12V-2×6 connector is on the top middle edge. Budget an angled 12V-2×6 adapter so it clears the side panel.
BIOSEnable Above 4G Decoding (mandatory for the 96 GB card's BAR) and Resizable BAR. Connect the board's PCIE_PWR1 supplemental 8-pin when running two GPUs.
🩺
Watch the motherboard, not just the GPUs. The best-documented failure in multi-GPU AI rigs is the board, not a cooked card. One builder chased daily lockups for weeks: the PCIe retimer chips hit 90°C and crashed the fabric while the GPUs — which don't throttle until ~95°C — reported themselves fine. Monitor board/VRM temps from day one.

Power-limit both GPUs (do this on day one)

For AI training, capping power barely dents throughput but slashes heat. Run after every reboot (or add to a startup script). Tune the wattages to taste.

power-limit.sh · run as admin/sudo
# See current limits & live draw
nvidia-smi -q -d POWER

# Cap each GPU (index 0 and 1). Adjust watts to your cards.
nvidia-smi -i 0 -pl 480   # RTX PRO 6000  (from 600W)
nvidia-smi -i 1 -pl 460   # RTX 5090      (from 575W)

# Watch temps under sustained load — target <80°C
nvidia-smi -q -d TEMPERATURE

# The stock fan curve idles at ~30% even at 85-90C. Override it.
# Linux: use LACT (github.com/ilya-zlobintsev/LACT) or a pynvml daemon.

🔌 Two power things people forget in India

  • Dedicated 16A circuit straight from the DB for the PC + AC. Don't share with the rest of the room. ~1.4kW PC + ~1.7kW (1.8-ton AC) ≈ 3.1kW continuous.
  • An online (double-conversion) UPS, ~3kVA. Training runs for hours — one power cut = lost progress + a hard yank on two ₹-heavy GPUs. Non-negotiable for a 24×7 training goal.

Your 1.8-ton O General inverter is the right call — with one condition. At full unthrottled power the room asks for ~1.9 ton, a touch over the unit's ~1.83-ton rating, so in peak summer it would run at high duty. The day-one GPU power-limit (~80%) drops the total to ~1.83 ton — right at its rating. Since you're power-limiting anyway (for heat + PSU headroom), the 1.8-ton is comfortably enough. O General is one of the best brands for 24×7 duty — its tropical-rated compressor holds temperature at 50°C+ ambient without throttling. Being an inverter, it modulates up under sustained load instead of short-cycling.

💸 Budget the running cost (it's real)

PC (~1.4kW) + 1.8-ton AC (~1.7kW) running 24×7 ≈ ~3.1 kW continuous ≈ ~75 units/day. At ₹8/unit that's roughly ₹600/day ≈ ₹18,000/month. Power-limiting, and only running training jobs when you need them, cuts this meaningfully.

7 Under the desk: will it become a hotbox? (No.)

A hot PC can cook itself in the wrong spot — but the "hotbox" failure mode is caused by enclosure, not by being under a desk. It happens in a closed cabinet cubby where the exhaust has nowhere to go and gets re-breathed (that can raise intake air ~20°C). Your open L-corner is the opposite of that.

✅ Why your setup is the good case

  • An open L-corner ≈ open air. Open on two sides, the exhaust vents freely into the room. Consensus is unambiguous: open back/sides is fine; only closed cubbies trap heat.
  • Intake and exhaust are far apart. Fresh air enters at the floor; heat leaves at the top — the length of the case apart. Hot air would have to travel all the way back down, against its own rising plume, to be re-breathed. It won't.
  • The floor is the coolest air in the room — the ideal feed for the flow-through 6000.
  • You have a real air gap above (~63–114 mm depending on pedestal) for the top exhaust to spread sideways rather than bounce back down.

🛠️ Do these

  • The wheeled pedestal: build it as an open frame (casters at the corners, open middle) — never a solid board, which would seal the floor intake and choke the 6000. Use locking casters rated well past ~50 kg.
  • Keep the two L-corner sides open and the desk a few inches off the wall — the exit path matters more than any gap number.
  • Aim the exhaust toward the AC's return so it scavenges the heat before it pools. Sit on the upstream/cool side.
  • Keep the bottom filter clean — it's the air path your 6000 breathes through. Leave a little cable slack so you can roll the case out to service it.
📌
Net: floor-intake + top-exhaust in an open L-corner, on a wheeled pedestal with an air gap above, is the recommended layout for a PC under a desk — not a risky one. The real lever for room heat is the AC, and the power-limit lands that inside your 1.8-ton O General's capacity. No hotbox.

9 Order of operations

  • Buy the V3000 Plus. Build (or buy) the open-frame wheeled pedestal, 1–2" tall, locking casters.
  • Have Ergosphere set the firmware minimum-height lock — measure the assembled case-on-pedestal, add ~50 mm + tabletop thickness, lock there (~30.5" for a 1" pedestal). Confirm it holds through a reset.
  • Fit the steel hard-stops at the same height as backup. Test by holding the down button.
  • Get the dedicated 16A circuit + 1.8-ton O General inverter AC installed.
  • Build: PRO 6000 in the LOWER slot (PCI_E2) over the floor + above-PSU intake fans, 5090 Astral LC in the top slot (PCI_E1). 5090 rad on the front (intake), LT720 on top (exhaust), rear fan exhaust.
  • BIOS: Above 4G Decoding on, Resizable BAR on, PCIE_PWR1 connected.
  • Verify link widths with GPU-Z — expect ×4 on the PRO 6000 (lower slot) and ×8 on the 5090 (top slot).
  • Install the fan-curve daemon and the power-limit script before the first sustained load.
  • Run a 2-hour training job. Watch GPU temps (<80°C) and motherboard/VRM temps.
  • Roll the PC under the desk. Position so the top exhaust vents toward the AC return, not your chair. Keep the corner sides open.
